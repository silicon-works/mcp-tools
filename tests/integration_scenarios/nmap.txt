## Scenario 2: Full port scan heartbeat regression (the 15.9% timeout scenario)
Method: port_scan
Args: {target: "<IP>", ports: "1-65535", scan_type: "syn", timing: "aggressive", skip_discovery: true}
Expected: success=true, completes without timeout
Notes: This IS Scenario 1 but the expected behavior is specifically about completion.
  33 of 207 method calls (15.9%) timed out. Two sessions (ses_2bf0 and ses_2d3e)
  had 50% timeout rates (12/24 and 11/24). The root cause was the MCP client
  killing connections after 300s with no heartbeat. Fixed with

## Scenario 64: FIN scan (stealth against non-stateful firewalls)
Method: port_scan
Args: {target: "<IP>", ports: "22,80,443,445", scan_type: "fin", timing: "aggressive", skip_discovery: true}
Expected: success=true, ports show as open|filtered or closed (FIN scans cannot
  definitively confirm open ports on most modern systems)
Notes: FIN scan (-sF) sets only the TCP FIN bit. RFC 793 says closed ports should
  respond with RST, while open ports should silently drop the packet. In practice,
  most Linux systems respond with RST for closed ports and nothing for open ports,
  making open/filtered indistinguishable. Windows ignores the RFC and sends RST
  regardless, so FIN scans are ineffective against Windows targets. Primary use:
  evading non-stateful packet-filtering firewalls and IDS that only look for SYN.
  Requires privileged mode.
# TEST RESULT: PASSED (2026-04-11, ultrasploit, Lame 10.129.213.227)
#   Results: Port 22 open|filtered, ports 80,443,445 filtered
#   Duration: 2.65 seconds
#   Status: Working correctly - FIN scan functional

## Scenario 65: Window scan (ACK-based open/closed distinction)
Method: port_scan

  filtered/unfiltered)
Notes: Window scan (-sW) is identical to ACK scan except it examines the TCP
